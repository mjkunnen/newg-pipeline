## IMAGE RULES (READ THIS FIRST)

### Dimension Rule
**Every single slide must be 1080x1920 pixels (9:16).** Your 1.6M post mixed 1170x2080, 1170x1188, and 900x1600 -- inconsistent sizing means some slides had black bars or awkward cropping. Lock in 1080x1920 for every slide, every post.

### The 3 Image Types You'll Use

**TYPE A: "Pinterest Moodboard" Images**
- Source: Pinterest, Tumblr, fashion editorial blogs
- What: Curated streetwear fit photos from other creators/photographers. Dark/moody tones, urban settings, archive-coded fits. NOT your products specifically.
- Why: This is what @god._.hand (114K followers, 100% slideshow) and @something.archive (97% slideshow) do. They curate aspirational imagery that builds a brand WORLD, not just a product catalog. These accounts get massive reach because the content feels like a magazine, not a shop.
- Editing: Run through a consistent filter (slightly desaturated, warm shadows, matte blacks). Use the SAME filter on every moodboard image so it looks curated, not random.
- Use for: Slide 1 hooks, lifestyle/aspiration slides, moodboard posts

**TYPE B: Real Product Photos (Your Own)**
- Source: Your phone, your products, your workspace
- What: Flat lays, detail shots, fabric close-ups, packaging shots, on-body photos of YOUR actual NEWGARMENTS pieces
- Why: This is the trust layer. Your audience needs to see the REAL product -- the fabric weight, the stitching, the packaging. Pinterest images build desire; your product photos close the deal.
- Editing: Clean, well-lit, same filter as Type A so everything feels cohesive. Natural window light. Clean surface (white/grey/wood/concrete).
- Quality rule: If the photo doesn't look good enough for an Instagram feed post, don't use it in a slideshow. Blurry, dark, or cluttered product photos kill conversions.
- Use for: Product reveal slides, detail/quality proof slides, packaging slides, CTA slides

**TYPE C: Text-Heavy Graphic Slides**
- Source: Created in Canva, Photoshop, or even TikTok's built-in editor
- What: Slides where text IS the content -- stats, breakdowns, quotes, comparisons
- Style: Dark background (black or very dark grey), clean white or off-white text, one bold statement per slide. Minimal. No clipart, no busy backgrounds, no gradients.
- Font: Bold sans-serif (Inter, Helvetica, Montserrat). Same font every time.
- Use for: Price breakdowns, GSM comparisons, customer quotes, CTA slides

### The Mix Per Slideshow
Most slideshows should use a combination:
- **Slide 1:** Type A (Pinterest moodboard) or Type B (striking product shot) -- this is the hook
- **Slides 2-4:** Mix of Type A and Type B depending on the concept
- **Slide 5 (CTA):** Type B (clean product shot) or Type C (text graphic)

Never make a slideshow that's ALL Type A (feels like a random Pinterest board, not your brand) or ALL Type C (feels like a PowerPoint, not content).

## IMAGE SOURCING GUIDE: WHERE TO FIND PINTEREST IMAGES

### Pinterest Search Terms for Type A Images
- "streetwear editorial photography dark"
- "archive fashion moody"
- "oversized hoodie urban photography"
- "streetwear fit dark aesthetic"
- "heavyweight streetwear lookbook"
- "underground fashion photography"
- "street style night photography"
- "minimalist streetwear editorial"

### What Makes a Good Type A Image
- Moody lighting (not bright/overexposed)
- Urban setting (concrete, brick, night streets, alleys, rooftops)
- Person wearing oversized/heavyweight clothing (should look like YOUR brand's vibe)
- Muted/desaturated colors (not bright neons or pastels)
- Confident body language (not posed/stiff, more candid)
- High resolution (min 1080px wide, ideally larger so you can crop to 9:16)

### What to AVOID in Type A Images
- Bright, colorful, "happy" aesthetics (doesn't match your brand)
- Recognizable brand logos from competitors (Nike swoosh, etc.)
- Images that are clearly from a different fashion category (preppy, formal, bohemian)
- Low resolution or watermarked images
- Images with text already on them

### Editing Type A Images
1. Open in any photo editor (Lightroom mobile is free, VSCO, Snapseed)
2. Reduce saturation slightly (-10 to -20)
3. Warm up shadows slightly
4. Add slight grain/texture
5. Increase contrast slightly
6. Apply the SAME preset to every Type A image -- consistency is everything

## SHOOTING YOUR OWN PRODUCT PHOTOS (TYPE B)

### Equipment
- Your phone (iPhone 12+ or Samsung S21+ ideal)
- A window (natural sidelight)
- One clean surface (options: white bedsheet, raw wood table, concrete floor, dark fabric draped flat)
- A cheap phone tripod or stack of books

### Flat Lay Setup
1. Place surface near a large window
2. Lay product flat, straighten all edges
3. Drape one sleeve off the surface edge (shows thickness/weight)
4. Shoot from directly above OR at 45-degree angle
5. Keep background clean -- remove EVERYTHING that isn't the product

### Detail/Macro Setup
1. Same window light, but get CLOSE (5-10cm from fabric)
2. Tap to focus on the detail (stitching, weave, label)
3. Make sure the detail is SHARP -- if it's blurry, retake
4. Fill the entire frame with the detail -- don't leave dead space

### On-Body Setup
1. Clean background (plain wall, simple room corner)
2. Natural light from the side
3. Full body: front, side, back
4. The heavyweight fabric should look HEAVY on camera -- if it looks thin, adjust lighting angle until you can see the texture and structure

### Packaging Setup
1. Clean surface (white or wood)
2. Overhead angle
3. Arrange: box, tissue paper unfolded, piece folded inside, handwritten note placed on top
4. Everything should be neat and intentional -- this needs to look like a gift

### Batch Shooting (60 minutes max)
- Shoot ALL flat lays first (20 min)
- Then ALL detail/macro shots (15 min)
- Then ALL packaging shots (10 min)
- Then ALL on-body shots (15 min)
- This gives you enough images for 3-4 slideshows
